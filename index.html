Graphic Design 3

Time + Motion + Grapic Design

GD300.05 Fall 2012

General GD3 Info

Catalog Description

Students actively engage motion graphics as strategic medium for experimentation, idea generation, problem solving and communication. Motion and interactivity are studied in the context of aesthetic, cultural, historical and critical issues. Students learn essential design processes and techniques in their exploration of time-based media both as a tool and as a medium for evolving designers.

Learning outcomes:

  1. Students utilize time as a design principle
  2. Students are provided an overview of designing for interactivity
  3. Students begin to develop proficiency and comfort in working with the tools for time-based media (flash, HTML5, After Effects, animated gifs, etc.)

Course Description

Design is increasingly moving from the printed page to the screen. Graphic Designers must be aware of and ready for the conceptual and technological implications of such a switch. The goals of this class are to clarify and explain such implications; cover the broader concepts of time and motion, and there connection to all facets of a design practice; and begin to introduce the software for creating this type of work, as well as non-software methods, tricks and techniques. Adobe Photoshop, Illustrator, and After Effects will be our main tools, but Flash and HTML5 / Javascript / CSS3, as well as Processing and some other tools will be discussed (and hopefully explored).

Historical and contemporary examples of designers using film, animation, and temporal tools and methods in their work will be referenced to help frame our discussions and projects. While software driven motion design will be a something we spend a lot of time on, we must not forget that much of the static design we make (like books, or billboards viewed at high speed) are time + motion experiences as well. As this is foremost a design class, emphasis will remain on making top-notch graphic design in concept and execution regardless of the medium.

Homework

For next week, please take what we’ve looked at and practiced today and complete more stop-motions (or rather, frame by frames) (in whatever format you desire) for next week … make them motion studies, try to use as many of the types of motion as you can, and integrate various design elements when possible: type, shapes, color, etc. Think about composition, form, balance, etc. all in the same way as you would for a traditional piece. Do not allow that this is a “motion” piece cause you to forget you are still making graphic design! My desire is this: 100 frames of carefully thought out animation. This could be simply one 4 second piece, or it could be ten 10 frames design-a-mations; the point is that all aspects of design, framing, motion, etc. have been thought out and carefully executed by you . . .

Look at things like “The Seed,” the “I am not an Artist” clips, and some of the other stop motion links I've attached for inspiration. Also consider what kind of design do you like to make, how can you make a short frame-by-frame animation that does similar things? What things are you interested in? Can you make a piece that integrates your other interests?

If you made your animation in photoshop, export it as an animated gif or as a movie—a simple .mov file would be fine; if you made it in imovie, export it as a simple .mov file; if you used Quicktime 7's sequence builder on one of the school computers, it will probably default to .mov as well

Lecture: Storyboards

The method used to sketch a temporal idea is a storyboard.

Storyboards outline the narrative and begin to develop compositional models for type, images, image frames, transitions, and sound(s).

Storyboards help you to both organize your thoughts and begin to indicate what is needed in order to actually produce the piece.

Storyboards do not necessarily outline every single frame; they merely include the more important “keyframes.” However, one must be sure to get a good range of frames illustrated so as to tell the story properly.

Storyboard frame outlines can be hand drawn, however, it is easier to generate the blank frames in the computer, print them out, and fill them in — or simply create blanks on the computer and fill them directly in in indesign, illustrator, or anything else you feel comfortable using.
